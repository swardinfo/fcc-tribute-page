Howard Florey

With penicillin he saved over 200 million lives

A brief history of Howard Florey

1898

Born Malvern Adelaide Australia, he was educated at Kyre College Preparatory School and then Saint Peter's College Adelaide where academically he excelled in chemistry and physics, but not mathematics.

1917

Studies medicine at the University of Adelaide, paid entirely by a state scholarship he won.

1924

Studying at Magdalen College Oxford as a Rhodes Scholar, he receives a BA degree.

1925

Left Oxford to study at Cambridge, wins a fellowship from the Rockefeller Foundation to study in the United States for ten months. A year after returning receives the degree of PhD.

1926

Married his first wife Mary Reed who also became his research colleague. Together they had two children.

1935

Returned to Oxford as Professor of Pathology and Fellow of Lincoln College Oxford leading a team of researchers. Working with Ernst Boris Chain, Norman Heatley, and Edward Abraham, he reads Alexander Fleming's paper discussing the antibacterial effects of Penicillium notatum mould.

1941

Florey and Chain treated their first patient Albert Alexander. Within a day of being given penicillin he started recovering, however the researchers did not have enough penicillin to help him to a full recovery and he relapsed and died.

1945

Florey's research team had improved the production of the mould and efficient extraction of the active ingredient, to the point where it had become an industrial process for treating the Allies in World War II.

1945

Florey shares the Nobel Prize in Medicine with Ernst Boris Chain and Alexander Fleming. Fleming first observed the antibiotic properties of the mould that makes penicillin, but it was Chain and Florey who developed it into a useful treatment.

1958

Florey opens the John Curtin School of Medical Research at the Australian National University in Canberra Australia.

1965

The Queen made him Lord Florey and he was offered and accepted the role of Chancellor of the Australian National University.

1967

After the passing of his first wife Mary in 1966, Florey marries long-time colleague and research assistant Margaret Jennings.

1968

Florey Dies at the age of 69.

"In terms of world well-being, Florey was the most important man ever born in Australia."

Australian Prime Minister
Sir Robert Menzies
